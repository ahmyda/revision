SLA Research Timeline

The Development of Second Language Acquisition Theories

Zebeir Ahmed & Labiad Hibat Allah Amina
Introduction

Language learning has always been an important part of human communication. Researchers have developed various theories to understand how people learn a second language.

Key theoretical perspectives include:

  • Behaviorism - focused on habit formation
  • Cognitivism - emphasized mental processing
  • Constructivism - highlighted active learning

Important hypotheses include Universal Grammar, Input Hypothesis, Interaction Hypothesis, Output Hypothesis, and Affective Filter Hypothesis.

Behaviorism
1940s-1950s
B.F. Skinner (1957)

Skinner equated language learning to verbal behavior. He believed language acquisition can be observed like any other behavior, not by explaining mental systems.

Children are born with a "blank slate" (tabula rasa) and acquire language through stimuli and conditioned responses via reinforcement.

Behaviorist Example
  • Child hears "I want milk" repeatedly
  • Child says "I want milk"
  • Adult gives milk (positive reinforcement)
  • Learning happens through repetition and reward

This view was challenged by Noam Chomsky in the 1950s-1960s, who argued behaviorism couldn't explain how children create new, grammatically correct sentences they've never heard before.

Universal Grammar
1950s-1960s
Noam Chomsky

Chomsky proposed humans are born with an innate Universal Grammar (UG) — a biological blueprint containing basic principles common to all languages.

Children don't learn language solely from experience; they activate and adjust UG parameters based on the language they're exposed to.

UG Example
  • Child hears "The dog chased the cat" once
  • Later, they correctly say "The cat chased the dog" without being taught
  • Shows understanding of grammar rules and ability to create new sentences

This forms the foundation of Generative Grammar, focusing on rules that allow humans to generate infinite sentences from finite elements.

Cognitivism
1960s-1970s
Jean Piaget

Piaget placed language acquisition within cognitive development. A child must understand a concept before acquiring the language form that expresses it.

Four stages of cognitive development:

  • Pre-language stage (3-10 months): Cooing and babbling
  • Holophrastic stage (12-18 months): Single words
  • Two-word stage (18-20 months): Two-word combinations
  • Telegraphic speech (before age 3): Short, simple sentences

Children's language reflects the development of their logical thinking and reasoning skills.

Constructivism
1970s-1980s
Lev Vygotsky & Jerome Bruner

Constructivism emerged as a reaction against behaviorism, emphasizing that learners actively construct knowledge using prior experiences.

Vygotsky highlighted the social nature of learning, introducing the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) — learners achieve more with guidance than alone.

Bruner promoted discovery learning, encouraging learners to explore language rules themselves rather than relying solely on explicit instruction.

Classrooms began focusing on authentic tasks: role-plays, dialogues, and project-based activities.

Krashen's Hypotheses
1970s-1980s
Stephen Krashen

Krashen proposed influential hypotheses focusing on the difference between acquiring a language naturally and learning it formally.

Acquisition-Learning

Acquisition occurs subconsciously through communication; learning is conscious study of rules.

Monitor Hypothesis

Learned knowledge acts as a monitor to correct language output.

Input Hypothesis

Learners progress with comprehensible input slightly beyond their level (i+1).

Natural Order

Language features are acquired in a predictable sequence.

Affective Filter

Emotions, motivation, and anxiety facilitate or block language acquisition.

Interaction Theories
1980s-1990s
Michael Long & Merrill Swain

Interaction-based theories emphasize that language learning occurs primarily through meaningful communication.

Long's Interaction Hypothesis: Learners acquire language more effectively when they encounter communication problems and work together to resolve them.

Interaction Example

Two students describing a picture in the target language may correct each other's mistakes or ask for clarification, helping them notice new language forms.

Swain's Output Hypothesis: Producing language helps learners recognize gaps in knowledge and internalize grammar.

Output Example

A student giving a presentation might realize they don't know the correct past-tense form and then seek help or self-correct.

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

SLA research shows evolution from behaviorist views (imitation/reinforcement) to cognitive and constructivist perspectives (mental processes, active learning, social interaction).

Chomsky's UG challenged behaviorism by highlighting innate linguistic knowledge. Piaget and Vygotsky stressed cognitive development and social context.

Krashen's and interaction-based hypotheses show that meaningful input, output, and collaborative communication are essential for effective acquisition.

Second language learning is a complex process involving cognitive, social, and emotional factors, with active engagement at its core.
