The Development of SLA Research Over Time

A Historical Timeline of Key Theories and Hypotheses
Zebeir Ahmed // Labiad Hibat Allah Amina
Introduction

Language Learning & Human Communication

Language learning has always been an important part of human communication. Over the years, researchers have tried to understand how people learn a second language and help them improve. Different theories have appeared, each offering a unique perspective on the learning process. These theories have shaped the approaches, methods, and techniques used to understand second language acquisition.

Key theoretical perspectives include:

  • Behaviorism - focused on habit formation
  • Cognitivism - emphasized mental processing
  • Constructivism - highlighted active learning

Important hypotheses include Universal Grammar, the Input Hypothesis, the Interaction Hypothesis, the Output Hypothesis, Sociocultural Theory, and the Affective Filter Hypothesis.

1940s-1950s

Behaviorism

B.F. Skinner (1957)

According to behaviorist theory, Skinner equated learning a language to verbal behavior. He believed that language acquisition, like any other behavior, can be observed rather than trying to explain the mental systems underlying these behaviors. Children are born with a "blank slate" (tabula rasa) and acquire L1 through stimuli and conditioned responses through reinforcement.

Behaviorist Example:
  • Child hears "I want milk" repeatedly and says it
  • Adult gives milk (reinforcement)
  • Learning happens through repetition and reward

This view was famously challenged by Noam Chomsky in the 1950s and 1960s. He argued that behaviorism could not explain how children produce new, grammatically correct sentences they've never heard before, since simple imitation and reinforcement cannot account for creativity in language.

1950s-1960s

Universal Grammar

Noam Chomsky

Chomsky proposed that humans are born with an innate Universal Grammar (UG) â€” a biological blueprint containing the basic principles common to all languages. According to this theory, children do not learn language solely from experience; instead, they activate and adjust the parameters of UG based on the specific language they are exposed to.

Universal Grammar Example:
  • Child hears "The dog chased the cat" once
  • Later, they correctly say "The cat chased the dog" without being taught
  • Shows understanding of grammar rules and ability to create new sentences

This idea forms the foundation of Generative Grammar (GG), which focuses on the underlying rules and structures that allow humans to generate an infinite number of sentences from a finite set of elements.

1960s-1970s

Cognitivism

Jean Piaget

Piaget placed language acquisition within the context of a child's cognitive development. He argued that a child has to understand a concept before they can acquire the language form that expresses that concept. He proposed four stages of cognitive development:

  • Pre-language stage (3-10 months): Cooing and babbling
  • Holophrastic stage (12-18 months): Single words
  • Two-word stage (18-20 months): Two-word combinations
  • Telegraphic speech stage (before age 3): Short, simple sentences

Piaget's theory states that children's language reflects the development of their logical thinking and reasoning skills in stages.

1970s-1980s

Constructivism

Lev Vygotsky & Jerome Bruner

Constructivism emerged as a reaction against behaviorist approaches, emphasizing that learners actively construct knowledge using their prior experiences. Vygotsky highlighted the social nature of learning, introducing the concept of the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD), where learners achieve more with guidance than alone.

Bruner promoted discovery learning, encouraging learners to explore language rules themselves rather than relying solely on explicit instruction. Classrooms began to focus on authentic, meaningful tasks such as role-plays, dialogues, and project-based activities.

Constructivist theory stressed that language learning is an active, social, and contextually situated process.

1970s-1980s

Krashen's Hypotheses

Stephen Krashen

Krashen proposed a series of influential hypotheses about second language acquisition, focusing on the difference between acquiring a language naturally and learning it formally.

Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis

Acquisition occurs subconsciously through meaningful communication, while learning is a conscious process of studying rules.

Monitor Hypothesis

Learned knowledge acts as a monitor to correct or edit language output.

Input Hypothesis

Learners progress when they receive comprehensible input slightly beyond their current level (i+1).

Natural Order Hypothesis

Language features are acquired in a predictable sequence, regardless of the learner's age or first language.

Affective Filter Hypothesis

Emotions, motivation, and anxiety play a crucial role in facilitating or blocking language acquisition.

1980s-1990s

Interaction-Based Theories

Michael Long & Merrill Swain

Interaction-based theories emphasized that language learning occurs primarily through meaningful communication.

Long's Interaction Hypothesis: Learners acquire language more effectively when they encounter communication problems and work together to resolve them, receiving comprehensible input in the process.

Interaction Example:

Two students trying to describe a picture to each other in the target language may correct each other's mistakes or ask for clarification, which helps them notice new language forms.

Swain's Output Hypothesis: Producing language helps learners recognize gaps in their knowledge and internalize grammar.

Output Example:

A student giving a short presentation in the second language might realize they don't know the correct past-tense form and then seek help or self-correct.

These examples show that active, social interaction promotes deeper language learning.

Conclusion

The development of second language acquisition research shows a clear evolution from behaviorist views focused on imitation and reinforcement, to cognitive and constructivist perspectives emphasizing mental processes, active learning, and social interaction.

Chomsky's Universal Grammar challenged behaviorism by highlighting innate linguistic knowledge, while Piaget and Vygotsky stressed cognitive development and social context. Krashen's and interaction-based hypotheses further demonstrated that meaningful input, output, and collaborative communication are essential for effective acquisition.

Overall, these theories reveal that second language learning is a complex process involving cognitive, social, and emotional factors, with active engagement and authentic use of language at its core.
