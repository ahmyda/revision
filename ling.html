Lecture 1: Introducing Applied Linguistics

Defining Applied Linguistics

Key Definitions

Applied Linguistics (AL) is not merely linguistics applied to real-world problems. It represents an interdisciplinary mediation between linguistic theory and practical language issues.

"Applied Linguistics is the theoretical and empirical investigation of real world problems in which language is a central issue."
— Brumfit (1977)

AL vs. Linguistics Applied

Linguistics Applied: Direct application of linguistic concepts to solve problems (unilateral approach).

Applied Linguistics: Mediation between linguistics and other perspectives (multilateral approach). Recognizes that language problems require interdisciplinary solutions.

The Scope of Applied Linguistics

1960s-1970s

AL primarily focused on language teaching, especially English as a foreign language.

1980s-Present

Expanded to include diverse areas: clinical linguistics, forensic linguistics, language planning, critical discourse analysis, and computational linguistics.

Real-World Applications
  • Language Education: Developing effective teaching methods for L1, L2, and foreign languages
  • Clinical Settings: Diagnosing and treating speech/language disorders
  • Legal Contexts: Analyzing language evidence in court cases
  • Workplace: Improving professional communication
  • Technology: Developing machine translation and NLP systems

Lecture 2: Contrastive Analysis (CA)

Theory of Contrastive Analysis

Core Principle

Contrastive Analysis (Lado, 1957) is the systematic comparison of L1 and L2 to predict learning difficulties based on structural differences.

Behaviorist Foundation: Language learning = habit formation. L1 habits interfere with L2 acquisition.

Example: Arabic vs. English
  • Arabic: No third-person present tense morpheme
  • English: Requires "-s" for third-person singular
  • Predicted Error: Arab learners say "he come" instead of "he comes"

Hierarchy of Difficulty

Stockwell's Hierarchy (1965)
  1. Split (Most Difficult): L1 has one form, L2 has two
    Arabic: /b/ vs English: /p/ and /b/
  2. New Category: Exists in L2 but not L1
    Arabic: no indefinite articles vs English: "a/an"
  3. Absent Category: Exists in L1 but not L2
    Arabic: grammatical gender vs English: natural gender
  4. Coalesced Forms: L1 has multiple, L2 has one
    Arabic: multiple negators vs English: "not"
  5. Complete Correspondence (Least Difficult)
    Simple past tense exists in both

Lecture 3: Error Analysis (EA)

Error vs. Mistakes

Key Differences
E
Errors
Systematic, reflect competence, cannot self-correct
M
Mistakes
Random performance slips, can self-correct
Error Examples
  • Interlingual: *"He student" (Arabic transfer: no copula)
  • Intralingual: *"goed" (Overgeneralization of -ed)
  • Developmental: *"I no like" (Early acquisition stage)

EA Methodology (8 Steps)

  1. Collect learner errors (compositions, tests)
  2. Identify deviations from L2 norms
  3. Classify error types
  4. Calculate frequency of each type
  5. Identify areas of difficulty
  6. Determine error sources
  7. Assess seriousness for communication
  8. Remedy through teaching interventions

Lecture 4: Interlanguage Theory

The Interlanguage Concept

Selinker's Definition (1972)

Interlanguage (IL) is a separate linguistic system between L1 and L2, created by learners through five cognitive processes:

  1. L1 Transfer - Influence of native language
  2. Transfer of Training - Effects of teaching methods
  3. Learning Strategies - Individual approaches to learning
  4. Communication Strategies - Ways of interacting in L2
  5. Overgeneralization - Extending L2 rules too far

Factors Affecting Strategy Choice

Influential Factors
  • Age: Children (task-specific) vs. Adults (generalized strategies)
  • Motivation: Highly motivated = more diverse strategies
  • Personality: Extroverts use more communication strategies
  • Learning Context: Classroom vs. natural settings
  • Task Type: Different strategies for listening vs. writing tasks
