SLA Research & Methods

Theories & Teaching Approaches Timeline
Zebeir Ahmed & Labiad Hibat Allah Amina
Introduction

Second Language Acquisition (SLA) research explores how people learn languages beyond their native tongue. This timeline shows the evolution of theories and teaching methods.

Theories evolved from behaviorist approaches to cognitive, constructivist, and interactionist perspectives.

Teaching methods like TPR, Silent Way, and Suggestopedia emerged from these theoretical foundations, applying principles to classroom practice.

Behaviorism
1940s-1950s
B.F. Skinner (1957)

Language learning as habit formation through stimulus-response-reinforcement. Children acquire language through imitation and reinforcement of correct forms.

Key Principle

Learning occurs through repetition, imitation, and positive/negative reinforcement of language behaviors.

Influenced Audiolingual Method (ALM) with pattern drills and memorization exercises.

Universal Grammar
1950s-1960s
Noam Chomsky

Humans have innate language faculty (Universal Grammar) with principles common to all languages. Children don't just imitate; they generate new sentences using internal grammar rules.

SLA Implication

Second language learners access UG parameters, explaining why some grammatical features transfer between languages while others don't.

Emphasized the creative, rule-governed nature of language acquisition over behaviorist imitation.

Cognitivism
1960s-1970s
Jean Piaget

Language acquisition depends on cognitive development. Children must reach certain cognitive milestones before acquiring corresponding language structures.

Four stages of cognitive development correspond to language development:

  • Sensorimotor → Pre-linguistic sounds
  • Preoperational → Vocabulary growth
  • Concrete operational → Complex sentences
  • Formal operational → Abstract language

For SLA: Learners' cognitive readiness affects what language they can process and produce.

Constructivism
1970s-1980s
Lev Vygotsky & Jerome Bruner

Learners actively construct knowledge through social interaction. Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): learners achieve more with expert guidance.

SLA Application

Collaborative tasks, peer feedback, and scaffolded instruction help learners internalize language within their ZPD.

Emphasized social, contextual nature of learning over individual cognitive processes.

Krashen's Hypotheses
1970s-1980s
Stephen Krashen

Five hypotheses explaining natural language acquisition vs. formal learning:

Acquisition-Learning

Subconscious acquisition through meaningful use vs. conscious learning of rules.

Input (i+1)

Comprehensible input slightly above current level drives acquisition.

Affective Filter

Low anxiety, high motivation facilitate acquisition; high anxiety blocks it.

Influenced Natural Approach and communicative language teaching.

Interaction Theories
1980s-1990s
Michael Long & Merrill Swain

Language acquisition occurs through negotiated interaction. When communication breaks down, learners notice gaps and adjust their language.

Long's Interaction Hypothesis: Modified interaction (clarification requests, comprehension checks) makes input comprehensible.

Swain's Output Hypothesis: Producing language pushes learners to process syntax and morphology deeply.

Classroom Practice

Information-gap activities, jigsaw tasks, and collaborative projects create opportunities for negotiated interaction.

Teaching Methods in SLA
1960s-1970s

These humanistic methods emerged as alternatives to grammar-translation and audiolingual approaches, applying SLA theories to practical teaching.

Total Physical Response (TPR)
James Asher (1960s)

Approach: Learners respond to commands with physical actions before speaking. Based on how children acquire L1: listening before speaking.

SLA Contributions
  • Reduces anxiety through physical activity
  • Develops listening comprehension before production
  • Embodies Krashen's "silent period" concept
  • Lowers affective filter through game-like activities
  • Connects language to meaningful physical actions
Silent Way
Caleb Gattegno (1970s)

Approach: Teacher says little; learners discover language through problem-solving with Cuisenaire rods and charts. Emphasizes learner autonomy.

SLA Contributions
  • Promotes discovery learning (Bruner)
  • Develops learner independence and self-correction
  • Focuses on pronunciation and structural patterns
  • Encourages hypothesis testing about language
  • Minimal teacher talk maximizes student production
Suggestopedia
Georgi Lozanov (1970s)

Approach: Uses relaxation, music, and positive suggestion to tap into learners' mental reserves. Creates "infantilization" for stress-free learning.

SLA Contributions
  • Directly addresses Krashen's Affective Filter
  • Uses relaxation to enhance memory retention
  • Integrates arts (music, drama) into language learning
  • Creates positive, confident learning environment
  • Demonstrates importance of psychological factors
Modern Legacy

These methods influenced contemporary approaches: TPR elements appear in young learner classes; Silent Way principles inform learner autonomy; Suggestopedia techniques survive in mindfulness and positive psychology approaches to language teaching.

Conclusion

SLA: From Theory to Practice

The evolution of SLA theories shows a shift from behaviorist habit-formation to cognitive, social, and affective perspectives on language learning.

Teaching methods like TPR, Silent Way, and Suggestopedia translated theoretical insights into classroom practices:

  • TPR applies natural acquisition order and reduces anxiety
  • Silent Way promotes discovery learning and autonomy
  • Suggestopedia addresses affective factors in acquisition

Modern SLA recognizes that effective language teaching integrates insights from multiple theories, addressing cognitive, social, and emotional dimensions of learning.
