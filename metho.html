Research Components in Didactics

Foundation

In didactics, research focuses on teaching and learning processes, aiming to improve educational outcomes through evidence-based strategies.

Component Didactics Focus Example
Research Problem Teaching challenges, learning gaps, engagement issues Why do learners struggle with speaking activities?
Research Questions Practical, process-oriented, open-ended What factors hinder oral participation?
Hypotheses Predict relationships between teaching variables Peer feedback improves writing more than teacher feedback
Problem Statement Contextualizes challenge and significance "Despite years of instruction, students avoid speaking due to anxiety..."

Didactics Research Scenario

Topic: Enhancing vocabulary retention through gamification

Gamification Cognitive Load Quantitative Qualitative

Research Question: How does gamified vocabulary instruction affect long-term retention compared to traditional methods?

Quantitative, Qualitative & Mixed Methods

Methodology
The choice between approaches should be rationalized based on research questions (Drake & Heath, 2011).

Comparing Approaches

Aspect Qualitative Quantitative
Goal Understand experiences Measure variables
Data Words, observations Numbers, statistics

Mixed Methods & Triangulation

Mixed Methods: Combining both approaches for comprehensive insights.

Triangulation: Using multiple methods to study the same phenomenon (Kadushin et al., 2008).

Real Didactics Study

Topic: Student engagement in online language learning

Quantitative Qualitative Mixed Methods

Design:

  1. Quantitative: Survey (engagement metrics, time spent)
  2. Qualitative: Focus groups (emotional experiences)
  3. Triangulation: Compare datasets for comprehensive understanding

Key Steps Explained

1. Identifying Research Problems

Start from broad interest → Narrow to specific, researchable question.

Example: "Online teaching during COVID-19" → "Effectiveness of synchronous vs asynchronous methods for vocabulary retention"

2. Literature Review

Not just summary—critical synthesis that identifies gaps and informs methodology.

3. Research Design

"Arrangement of conditions for collection and analysis of data" (Claire Selltiz, 1960).

Philosophical Lens

Philosophy of Research (Worldviews)

Paradigms
A worldview is "a basic set of beliefs that guide action" — your philosophical lens shapes how you see and study educational phenomena.

Research Worldviews

Worldview Core Belief Didactics Application
Positivism Objective, measurable reality Testing teaching methods with experiments
Interpretivism Socially constructed knowledge Understanding student experiences
Critical Theory Power, inequality, change Examining educational equity
Pragmatism Practical problem-solving Solving classroom challenges
Constructivism Multiple constructed realities How learners build knowledge

Worldview in Action

Research Topic: Student anxiety in language learning

Positivist Approach:
Measure anxiety levels with surveys, test interventions
Interpretivist Approach:
Interview students about their lived experiences
Critical Approach:
Examine how assessment systems create anxiety

Data Collection & Analysis Tools

Practical Tools
The right tools depend on your research questions, approach, and context. Didactics research often uses mixed tools.

Qualitative Tools

Interviews

Structured, semi-structured, unstructured

Focus Groups

8-10 participants, guided discussion

Observations

Direct, indirect, participant

Case Studies

In-depth analysis of bounded cases

Quantitative Tools

Surveys

Closed-ended questionnaires

Experiments

Lab, field, natural, quasi

Tests

Pre/post tests, assessments

Complete Didactics Study Design

Topic: "Effect of peer feedback on writing skills in EFL classrooms"

Data Collection:
  • Pre-test & post-test writing samples
  • Feedback rubrics
  • Student surveys
  • Teacher interviews
Analysis Tools:
  • SPSS for scores
  • Thematic analysis for feedback
  • Excel for basic stats
Ethical Considerations:
  • Informed consent
  • Confidentiality
  • Voluntary participation

Research Typology

Types of Research in Didactics

Classification
Research
Types

Pure Research

Knowledge for its own sake, theoretical foundations.

Example: Cognitive processes in SLA

Applied Research

Solving real-world educational problems.

Example: Testing new teaching tools

Descriptive

Profiles, classifications, who/what/where.

Example: Student demographic analysis

Correlational

Relationships between variables.

Example: Attendance vs. grades

Explanatory

Why things happen, causality.

Example: Why method X works

Exploratory

New topics, initial understanding.

Example: Teachers' AI attitudes
